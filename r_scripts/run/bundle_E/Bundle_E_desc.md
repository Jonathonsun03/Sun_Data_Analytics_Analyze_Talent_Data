## Bundle E: Video Lifecycle and Library Growth

### 1) Who this report is for
- Talent managers who want to understand whether a talent's library is compounding over time or relying mostly on new uploads.
- Talent/creators who want to know which videos keep growing after release, which ones fade quickly, and which content patterns create longer-tail performance.

### 2) What this bundle answers
- Which videos are still growing after publish, and which ones plateau quickly?
- Is the channel's performance increasingly driven by the back catalog, recent uploads, or a healthy mix of both?
- Which videos show evergreen behavior versus short-lived spike behavior?
- How quickly do videos accumulate views over key age windows such as first 7, 30, 60, and 90 days?
- Which content types, topics, tags, or collaboration patterns are associated with better long-tail growth?
- Are there videos or content cohorts that re-accelerate after their initial release?
- Is the library becoming more durable over time, with more videos continuing to contribute meaningful views after launch?

### 5) Included analyses (full scope)
1. Overall snapshot:
Library growth summary, total tracked videos, total library views over time, share of views from recent vs back-catalog videos, and concentration of views across the library.
2. Video life cycle:
Per-video growth curves, age-normalized view accumulation, early velocity vs later durability, and identification of plateauing versus still-growing videos.
3. Cohort analysis:
Performance by publish month or publish period, comparing how groups of videos age and accumulate views over similar life stages.
4. Evergreen analysis:
Ranking of videos by long-tail contribution, sustained growth rate, and consistency of contribution after initial release windows.
5. Content pattern analysis:
Longitudinal performance by content type, topic, tag, and collaboration status to identify which creative patterns hold value over time.
6. Opportunity scan:
Sleeper videos, resurging videos, and videos with unusually strong late-stage growth that may warrant repromotion or follow-up content.

### 6) Strategic use
- Helps managers separate short-term upload wins from durable library-building wins.
- Helps creators identify formats that keep paying off after release instead of only generating launch-week spikes.
- Supports repromotion, sequel planning, playlist strategy, clipping strategy, and editorial decisions around evergreen content.

### 7) Interpretation guardrails
- This report is observational, not causal. A video's long-tail growth may reflect promotion, seasonality, platform recommendations, external events, or catalog placement.
- Video age matters. Older videos have had more time to accumulate views, so age-normalized comparisons should be prioritized when comparing across uploads.
- Three months of longitudinal data is enough to show early life-cycle patterns, but not full lifetime behavior for older evergreen conclusions.
- Re-acceleration can be meaningful, but it should be interpreted carefully because it may reflect one-off boosts rather than durable renewed demand.
- Topic and tag analyses are directional because metadata can overlap and performance can be influenced by factors outside the labeled content attribute.

### 8) Data requirements
- Repeated video-level snapshots over time must be available so the same video can be observed across multiple collection dates.
- A stable video identifier is required to connect the same upload across snapshots.
- Analytics staging data must retain observation dates, publish dates, and video-level view totals.
- Title classification or equivalent enrichment should be available for content-type, topic, tag, and collaboration analysis.
- A minimum longitudinal window of roughly 8-12 weeks is recommended for meaningful life-cycle interpretation.

### 9) Bundle E concept summary
- Bundle A answers broad performance and growth questions at the talent level.
- Bundle B answers content strategy and optimization questions at the format/topic level.
- Bundle E extends both by answering how individual videos and the full library develop over time.
- The core idea is to move from "what performed well" to "what kept performing well, for how long, and in what pattern."
